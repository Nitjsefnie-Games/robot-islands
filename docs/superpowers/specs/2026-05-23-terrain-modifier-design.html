Terrain Modifier — building the only catalog stub that needs a whole new mechanic

Context terrain_modifier (defined at src/building-defs.ts:1395) is the last surviving catalog stub from the building-removal audit — every other unfinished building was either deleted or wired by adding a recipe row. This one survived because per-tile terrain conversion does not exist in the game yet. SPEC §8.9 reserves the building for "clears or converts tiles," and the def's own comment says the mechanic "ships as a catalog row" — i.e. the UI action is intentionally deferred. This spec proposes that action.
Load-bearing finding from research Tile terrain today is closure-only. spec.terrainAt(x, y) is a function attached by attachTerrainAt at construction (src/world.ts:252); it dispatches to terrainAtForBiome(biome, id, x, y, inscribed) (src/biomes.ts:581) which is deterministic over (biome, islandId). Persistence literally strips the closure at save time (src/persistence.ts:165: Omit<IslandSpec, 'terrainAt'>) and rebuilds it on load. Neither Universe Editor nor Land Reclamation Hub writes a single tile — they mutate biome / radii and let the closure re-roll. Per-tile mutation is a new architectural component (a sparse tileOverrides map on IslandSpec); the cost is largely fixed across the picker options below, only when / how that map gets written varies.

What this v5 decides
Terrain Modifier is a single-shot, self-destroying, duplication-with-investment building: pay massive resources to instantiate a 16-tile patch of a player-chosen biome (including rare veins), modifier self-destroys, and the converted patch yields at the natural rate over its lifetime. Cost is tuned so manufacturing is a long-term investment, never a short-term profit.
All v1 / v2 / v3 picks baked. Every phase-1, phase-2, and phase-3 picker's default reflects what the user confirmed. Phase-1: single_shot, building_choice, all_tiles_including_water, power_plus_resource, reversible_via_modifier (reframed — see locks), animated_N_seconds, independent, adjacent_only (reinterpreted — see locks), map_click_mode, stack_rate, block_if_occupied. Phase-2: shot_at_placement, stack_count, placement_cost_only, place_second_modifier (reframed), accept_16_tiles_footprint_plus_ring, inside_ellipse_only, upfront_cost, full_brush_charge, building_arc, abort_if_any_occupied, plus auto_restore_original (now obsoleted). Phase-3: convert_and_modifier_sinks (obsoleted), skip_outside_full_charge, any_tile_including_veins, single_level_original_only (obsoleted), silent_no_op_for_those_tiles (obsoleted), last_placed_wins, history_persists_on_island (obsoleted). Phase-4: parity_with_natural (v5 lock).
v3 user locks (still in force). (1) Scope is the modifier's own 2×2 footprint + the 12-tile adjacent ring = 16 tiles total. (2) The sole “revert” path is placing a second terrain_modifier — reinterpreted by v5: that's just another forward conversion (see v5 lock 3 below).
v4 user lock (still in force). rare_target_costs_rare_resource — targets in the rare-vein set cost a heavy stack of the corresponding resource. Formula in §04: cost = K × natural_extraction_rate × payback_horizon, with Appendix-A placeholders K = 1.5, payback_horizon = 90 cycles. Anti-cheese: the manufactured vein cannot net-produce its own resource at a profit; it's a long-term investment.
v5 user locks (the simplification round). (1) modifier_self_destroys_after_shot — the modifier is removed entirely after firing; not “inert,” gone. Footprint tiles are freed for other buildings. Resolves the “does the modifier sink in water?” and “what if the player demolishes the inert modifier?” questions by deleting their premise. (2) rare_target_costs_rare_resource — v4 lock, recapped here as part of the v5 simplification bundle (it makes any_tile_including_veins safe). (3) no_revert_mechanic — there is NO revert path. “Reverting” a tile means placing another modifier whose building_choice target happens to be what you want; it's a normal forward conversion. tileOverrides stores only the current kind, no originalType, no history stack. Resolves the override-history-shape question and the revert-on-no-history question by deleting them.

Player progression framing · v5 narrative

Resources stay discovery-locked at first contact. The game's natural progression is unchanged — you must still find a rare vein on an island before you have any of that resource. Once you have some, you have two complementary distribution mechanisms:

  1. Routes — move the resource between islands as inventory cargo (existing mechanic).
  2. Terrain Modifier — spend a heavy stack of the resource itself to manufacture new vein tiles, either on the same island (densify your access) or on another island you've routed enough of the resource to (seed it for local extraction).

Terrain Modifier doesn't create resources from nothing — it amplifies what you already have. A player who has never found a diamond vein cannot manufacture one (they have no diamond ore to pay the input cost). A player who has discovered one can scale up. This keeps the early-game discovery loop intact while giving mid- and late-game players a capital-investment lever for resource throughput on islands that nature didn't gift them. The cost formula that enforces this lives in §04.

01

Tile / biome data model — closure today, sparse-override tomorrow

What you'd be touching. The bullets are research findings with file:line citations; the prose below interprets them.

There are 34 distinct TerrainKind values defined at src/island.ts:15-59 — from the original 5 (grass, stone, ore, coal, water) through the step-8 biome palette (tree, sand, ice, magma_vent) up to the T3 mineral veins added in Phase 10 and the §6.4 uranium_vein. Each carries a color in TERRAIN_COLOR (lines 71-109). The kinds split intuitively into buildable defaults (grass, sand, stone per biome), open obstacles (water, tree, ice, magma_vent), and the long tail of geological resources (every _vein / _pit / _deposit / _well / _seep).

Terrain is computed from a function, not stored as an array. IslandSpec.terrainAt?: (x, y) => TerrainKind (src/world.ts:156) is the only place an island carries its terrain. attachTerrainAt (src/world.ts:252-263) binds the closure to terrainAtForBiome(spec.biome, spec.id, x, y, inscribed) — and it captures spec by reference, so any in-place biome flip (Universe Editor) makes the closure re-roll the terrain on the next call. There is no per-tile storage anywhere.

Universe Editor flips spec.biome in place (line 86) and lets the closure re-roll; Land Reclamation grows spec.majorRadius / minorRadius by 1 (line 134-138). Neither writes a tile.

Placement gates on def.requiredTile (src/placement.ts:226-234): every footprint tile must return a TerrainKind in the allowed list, queried via spec.terrainAt(t.x, t.y). This is the only path by which terrain affects gameplay — a Mine demands every tile of its footprint be ore or coal; an Oil Pump demands every tile be oil_well; etc. The terrain modifier's job is precisely to unblock these checks by overwriting the offending tile.

Persistence at src/persistence.ts:163-165: SerializedIslandSpec = Omit<IslandSpec, 'terrainAt'>. The closure is recomputed on load. Anything per-tile that terrain_modifier needs to remember across saves has to live in a new field on IslandSpec that JSON-survives.

What this means for the implementation
Add tileOverrides?: Record<string, TerrainKind> (or Map<string, TerrainKind> serialized as a pair array) to IslandSpec. Key format "x,y" in island-local tile coords. Thread through attachTerrainAt so the closure first checks tileOverrides[\`${x},${y}\`] and falls back to terrainAtForBiome. Persist via the existing spread in serializeWorld — no schema bump required if the field is optional. This part of the spec doesn't change based on the pickers below.

Open design choices — pick & send back

Eleven fieldsets. Each one is a fork that meaningfully changes how the building plays or how the implementation slices. Defaults reflect the path-of-least-architectural-surprise. Click any other option to redirect — the Selections panel at the bottom updates live and a copy button hands you the payload to paste back. Picker 6 (time / animation) interacts with Picker 1: the per_tick_passive sub-option only makes sense if Picker 1 is set to passive_radius; pick those two consistently.

Picker 01
Mechanic shape — what is "tile conversion"?
The biggest fork. Determines whether the building is a passive ambient effect or an explicit player action. Every option is implementable; they differ in pacing, interactivity, and how UI-heavy the feature gets.
Picker 02
Conversion target — what can it produce?
Controls how flexible the building is. The more arbitrary the target, the more UI surface ("pick a destination biome") and the more potential for player abuse (turn the whole island into oil_well tiles? probably not the design intent).
Picker 03
Source restrictions — which tile types CAN be converted?
The complement to Picker 02. What is protected from conversion? Veins and special-resource tiles are the big question: if a player can paint over an ore vein, they've lost a permanent geological resource and may not realise until much later.
Picker 04
Cost — what does one conversion cost?
The def already declares power: { consumes: 100 } (line 1403) and placementCost: { steel: 50, gear: 20 }. Pick the per-tile / ongoing economic loop on top of that.
Picker 05
Reversibility — can a conversion be undone? v5 reframe
v5 reframe: there is no true revert mechanic (see v5 lock no_revert_mechanic). The user's pick reversible_via_modifier stays as the default for payload audit, but its meaning collapsed: a player “reverts” a tile by placing a second terrain_modifier whose building_choice target is whatever they want the tile to become. It's just another forward conversion; the engine has no notion of “the previous type.” The radio value is preserved so v3 payloads round-trip; the prose below is rewritten.
Picker 06
Time / animation — instant or animated?
Conversion duration. per_tick_passive only makes sense if Picker 01 = passive_radius; the rest pair with any active shape.
Picker 07
Integration with land_reclamation_hub
Land Reclamation grows the island ellipse (radius +1, currently 5 × r² stone per increment, capped at BIOME_MAX_RADII in world.ts:108). Terrain Modifier mutates per-tile. Are they orthogonal, coupled, or does one subsume the other?
Picker 08
Scope — what area can one building affect?
The modifier's footprint is fixed at 2×2. Reach is separate. Smaller reach = more buildings per island (multiple modifiers, see Picker 10). Larger reach = one modifier covers the whole island. v3 user override: adjacent_only is reinterpreted to mean the 2×2 footprint itself + the 12-tile adjacent ring = 16 tiles total. v5 user lock: the modifier self-destroys after firing (per modifier_self_destroys_after_shot), so the 4 footprint tiles convert and then the building is gone — no “modifier sinks / scorches” sub-question. See §03-B phase-2 picker 5 for the 16-tile expansion and §03-C phase-3 picker 1 (now obsoleted) for the prior debate.
Picker 09
UI surface — where does the player invoke it?
Only relevant if Picker 01 is an active_* shape. The two prior terrain-mutating buildings split: Land Reclamation Hub uses inspector buttons; Universe Editor uses inspector biome-grid buttons. There's no existing map-click-mode pattern in the codebase.
Picker 10
Multi-building stacking — two modifiers on one island
What happens if a player places two terrain_modifiers on the same island?
Picker 11
Buildings sitting on a converted tile — policy
If a player tries to convert a tile that a building already covers (or whose requiredTile is about to be broken), what happens? Universe Editor's existing policy at universe-editor.ts:91-110 is “convert anyway, mark the building invalid: true.” The terrain_modifier mechanic is more point-precise so it has more options.

04

Things deliberately NOT in the pickers

Decisions the implementer can make mechanically once the pickers are resolved — surfaced here so you know they were considered, not missed.

Tier / cost-floor. The def is already T2 with placementCost: { steel: 50, gear: 20 } and power.consumes: 100. Tier-2-appropriate; no change recommended. Tier moves should be a separate spec.

Persistence schema bump. Adding tileOverrides? as an optional field on SerializedIslandSpec does NOT require a save-version bump — the spread in serializeWorld (persistence.ts:265-279) and the rehydration loop both survive missing-optional fields. Legacy saves load as empty overrides map.

Render path. After mutation, call rebuildWorldLayers() from main.ts:328 — the same exit Universe Editor and Land Reclamation already use. renderIsland reads tiles through spec.terrainAt every rebuild; once attachTerrainAt's closure consults tileOverrides, the painted tile shows up automatically. No render-layer changes needed.

Hover-tooltip wording. The existing hover-tooltip.ts reads spec.terrainAt(x, y) already, so converted tiles report their new kind. v5 update: under the no_revert_mechanic lock the engine doesn't remember the original kind, so there's no (was: tree) annotation to surface. If we want one, the renderer can re-derive the seed-original at hover time by calling terrainAtForBiome(biome, id, x, y, inscribed) directly (the same closure backing terrainAt) and comparing — cheap, no new state.

Tests. A new src/terrain-modifier.test.ts mirroring land-reclamation.test.ts would cover the pure mutation primitive. The closure-by-reference invariant test in biomes.test.ts still holds — we only ADD a precedence step (overrides first), don't change the closure capture.

SPEC.md amendment. §8.9 row already says “clears or converts tiles” — that's compatible with any picker choice. The spec change is the data model addition (mention tileOverrides sparse map under §3.4 terrain or §3.7 placement). Small append, no semantics drift.

Cost-formula tuning — natural-biome targets. For targets in the “natural” set (grass, sand, stone, water, tree, ice, magma_vent) the cost is a flat per-tile basket. Appendix-A placeholder: { stone: 5, gear: 2 } per tile × 16 tiles = { stone: 80, gear: 32 } per shot. Mirrors Land Reclamation's stone-only-placeholder shape; tune after first-cut play.

Cost-formula tuning — rare-vein targets (v4 lock). When building_choice picks a target that is a rare vein / special deposit (any _vein / _pit / _deposit / _well / _seep / ore / coal / limestone entry), the basket is computed by:

v5 cost formula · the duplication-glitch framing

cost(rare_target) = K × natural_extraction_rate × payback_horizon

where natural_extraction_rate is the per-cycle yield of the natural extractor on a natural tile of the target kind, payback_horizon is the number of cycles before the player breaks even, and K ≥ 1 is the tuning lever (K = 1 = exact break-even at the horizon; K > 1 = net-negative until well past it).

Appendix-A placeholders: K = 1.5, payback_horizon = 90 cycles (≈ 30–60 minutes of real play depending on cycle time). The FORMULA is the v5 lock; the numbers are tunable.

Why this shape. The mechanic functions as a deliberate duplication-with-investment loop: pay a big upfront stack of resource X, manufacture an X-vein tile, place an X-extractor on it, and recover X at the natural rate until it net-exceeds the input. It's a duplication glitch with the numbers turned the other way — massive upfront, slow payback. The player can never bootstrap a resource they've never discovered (no X to pay the input) — the mechanic amplifies what you have, it doesn't create what you don't. See the “Player progression framing” callout near §01 for the narrative.

v5 lock summary — three plain-prose clarifications.

(1) modifier_self_destroys_after_shot — the building is removed entirely once its 16-tile shot completes; footprint tiles freed; no inert artefact persists.

(2) rare_target_costs_rare_resource — targets in the rare-vein set cost a heavy stack of the corresponding resource (formula above); natural targets stay on the flat per-tile basket.

(3) no_revert_mechanic — there's no special “revert” path. Every modifier placement is a forward conversion to whatever target the player picked. To change a converted tile back, place a new modifier with the desired target as another normal forward shot. tileOverrides stores only the current kind — no originalType, no history.
